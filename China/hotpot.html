Chinese Cuisine

HotPot

Hotpot is a popular and social style of dining in China, where fresh ingredients are cooked together in a pot of simmering broth placed at the centre of the table. Common ingredients include thinly sliced meats, seafood, vegetables, tofu, and noodles, allowing diners to cook food to their own preference. Hotpot is especially enjoyed during colder seasons and is known for bringing family and friends together through shared meals and lively conversations.

China is home to many hotpot styles, each with its own unique flavours. Two of the most well-known are Chongqing hotpot, famous for its spicy and numbing broth, and Beijing hotpot, which features a lighter, clearer soup that highlights the natural taste of the ingredients. Explore these popular hotpot varieties to discover the different flavours and traditions behind this beloved Chinese dining experience.

Chongqing hotpot is one of the most famous and distinctive hotpot styles in China, known for its rich, fiery broth and bold flavours. Originating from Chongqing in southwestern China, this hotpot is characterised by a deep red soup base made with dried chillies, Sichuan peppercorns, garlic, and spices, creating a taste that is both spicy and numbing. The broth is usually simmered with beef tallow, giving it a strong aroma and intense flavour.

Diners cook a wide variety of ingredients in the bubbling broth, such as thinly sliced beef, pork, vegetables, tofu, and noodles. Chongqing hotpot is especially popular for its exciting heat and warming effect, making it a favourite choice for those who enjoy spicy food and lively shared meals with friends and family.

Beijing hotpot is a traditional Chinese hotpot style known for its light, clear broth that brings out the natural flavours of fresh ingredients. Unlike spicier hotpot varieties, Beijing hotpot focuses on simplicity and balance, often using a mild soup base made from water, ginger, spring onions, and sometimes goji berries or jujubes. This gentle broth allows diners to enjoy the original taste of meats and vegetables without strong seasoning.

Common ingredients include thinly sliced lamb or beef, leafy vegetables, tofu, mushrooms, and glass noodles. Beijing hotpot is typically enjoyed with a variety of dipping sauces, such as sesame paste, garlic, and soy sauce, which add flavour according to personal preference. This hotpot style is popular for family gatherings and is appreciated for its comforting taste and warm, communal dining experience.
